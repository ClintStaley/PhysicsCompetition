Overview

The STEM competition website offers STEM design competitions with the following common elements. Several specific examples are below.


  1. Automated generation of STEM design challenges of various types.

  2. Required use of mathematical/scientific principles to precisely predict the performance of a submitted design.

  3. Online animation showing a test of the design, with precise quantitative results.

  4. An opportunity to improveme the design, but with requirements and limits that preclude mere cut-and-try development without deeper analysis.

  5. Random generation of problem parameters, to provide an unlimited number of design challenges of the same type.

  6. (Optional) Team work and/or team competitions


The website provides the student with a description, both textual and diagrammatic, of a design challenge, and allows the student to enter a solution, including an exact description of the expected behavior of the solution, derived from mathematical analysis of the problem. The site responds with a simulated run of the design, generally in the form of a sketch-animation of the design’s performance, with associated quantitative information regarding the design run. The student may then resubmit an improved design, up to some limit of repetitions.

How Does This Differ from Similar Sites

There are other STEM design-competition websites, most notably ...., which closely imitates traditional physics design competitions like model plane design or bridge building, with an added element of CAD-style design. XXX differs from these in several respects

  1. A focus on quantitative, rather than qualitative, design and analysis. Physical competitions, and the virtual competitions offered by sites like ...., both teach broad principles, e.g. that some bridge members are under tension while others are under compression. But students do not generally analyze their designs using mathematical principles, nor arrive at precise and verifiable predictions of design performance. Each XXX design competition requires mathematical analysis, and offers students the rewarding experience of predicting an outcome using scientific and engineering principles, and seeing their predictions prove numerically accurate.

  2. Smaller challenges, appropriate for large homework assignments, rather than multiweek projects. Smaller challenges allow a faster learning cycle, and allow time for several different design challenges, empasizing different concepts.

  3. Available without charge
