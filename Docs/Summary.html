Overview

The proposed competition website offers STEM design competitions with the following common elements. A specific example is below.


  1. STEM design challenges of various types.

  2. Required use of mathematical/scientific principles to precisely predict the performance of a submitted design.

  3. Online animation showing a test of the design, with precise quantitative results.

  4. An opportunity to improve the design, but with requirements and limits that preclude mere cut-and-try development without use of mathematical principles.

  5. Random generation of parameters, to provide an unlimited number of design challenges of the same type.

  6. (Optional) Team work and/or team competitions


The website provides the student with a description, both textual and diagrammatic, of a design challenge, and asks the student to enter a solution, including a numerical description of the expected behavior of the solution, derived from mathematical analysis of the problem.


The site responds with a simulated run of the design, generally in the form of a sketch-animation of the design’s performance, with associated quantitative information regarding the design run.


The student may then resubmit an improved design, up to some limit of repetitions.


The site provides summaries of student participation and performance to the instructor.


Example Competition


Bounce

The Bounce competition asks the student to use basic kinematics, including gravitational acceleration, to hit a set of targets by rolling a ball from a platform. Here's a sample design scenario:






The diagram shows several balls, and a ramp of 4m height. The student chooses how high on the ramp to place a ball. The ball rolls down the ramp and off the ledge, without friction.




If it strikes any of the platforms (solid black lines) below the ledge, it bounces elastically, and the platform disappears.




A ball may strike multiple platforms if it's rolled at the right launch speed. The goal is to cause all platforms to disappear (to be struck by a ball) using as few balls as possible. The platforms are exactly positioned in a coordinate space, and gravitational acceleration is assumed to be 9.8 m/s, so students can precisely calculate the speed needed to hit several platforms with the same ball, and the ramp-height needed to give the ball that speed.

Balls may also be launched by setting them at the edge of the ledge, and using another ball to strike them, again with a fully elastic collision. Balls have varying masses, as labelled, and thus greater speed might be obtained by such collisions than by ramp-rolling alone.




This basic challenge covers a number of concepts:

  • Gravitational acceleration

  • Translation of potential energy to velocity and back, when computing ramp height and determining height of bounces

  • Velocity components (vertical component reversed by a bounce but horizontal unchanged)

  • Elastic collisions

Once students have mastered the basic challenge, it can be extended in a number of ways:

  • Partially elastic collisions

  • Angled platforms

  • Horizontal acceleration during ball flight due to "wind"

  • Multiball collisions to increase speed






How Does This Differ from Similar Sites?

There are other STEM design-competition websites, most notably www.whiteboxlearning.com, which closely imitates traditional physics design competitions like model plane design or bridge building, with an added element of CAD-based design. This proposal differs in two important respects

  1. We plan a focus on quantitative design and analysis. Physical competitions, and the virtual competitions offered by sites like Whitebox Learning, both teach broad principles, e.g. that some bridge members are under tension while others are under compression. But students do not generally analyze their designs and arrive at precise and verifiable predictions of design performance. Our design competitions offer the educational experience of predicting an outcome using scientific and mathematical principles, and seeing their predictions prove numerically accurate.

  2. We propose smaller challenges, appropriate for large homework assignments, rather than multiweek projects. Smaller challenges allow a faster learning cycle, and might permit several different design challenges, empasizing different concepts.
